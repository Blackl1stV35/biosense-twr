# BioSense TWR — Technology Benchmark

This document compares the BioSense TWR PoC against the landscape of
through-wall and through-object sensing technologies. It serves as a
**read-only reference** — do not modify as part of the simulation pipeline.

Muon tomography and quantum gravimetry appear here as **physics-limit benchmarks**:
technologies that define the theoretical ceiling for penetration depth and
material independence, but which are not feasible for real-time human
detection at the scan times and form factors required by the PoC goal.

## Muon tomography — physics-limit benchmark

### What it achieves

Cosmic-ray muons (produced at ~15 km altitude, energy ~1–10 GeV) penetrate
hundreds of metres of rock or concrete with no attenuation limit. By placing
detector panels above and below a volume of interest and measuring the
scattering angles of muons passing through, a density map is recovered.

- **Penetration**: unlimited — muons traverse entire buildings, volcanoes, pyramids
- **Material independence**: works through reinforced concrete, steel, lead
- **Spatial resolution**: ~cm-scale with sufficient statistics
- **Flux**: ~10,000 muons/m²/min at sea level

### Why it cannot replace UWB for real-time human detection

| Constraint | Value | Impact |
|---|---|---|
| Scan time for 10 cm resolution | 30–60 min | Cannot detect moving humans |
| Detector size | 18 m² prototype | Not portable |
| Flux density at human scale | ~1,000 muons/m²/min (human cross-section) | Statistical noise floor |
| Cost | Research-grade detectors, ~$50k–500k | Not field-deployable |

**Benchmark role**: defines the theoretical penetration ceiling. Any UWB system
operating beyond a few walls in reinforced concrete must accept fundamental
SNR limits that muon imaging does not face. The gap between UWB and muon
capability in dense environments quantifies the unsolved engineering problem.

### Rust reference implementation

`poc/rust_kernels/src/muon_density.rs` implements the PoCA (Point of Closest
Approach) reconstruction algorithm. It is retained as a reference kernel for
future hybrid experiments but is **not exported** in the PoC pipeline.

## Quantum gravimetry — physics-limit benchmark

### What it achieves

Atom interferometry measures absolute gravity with sensitivity to
sub-nanogal (1 nGal = 10⁻¹¹ m/s²) variations. Because gravity is
generated by mass and penetrates all materials without any attenuation,
it is the only known physical observable that is truly material-independent.

- University of Birmingham (2022): detected a 2×2 m tunnel at 0.5 m depth
- Commercial quantum gravimeters: sensitivity ~10 nGal/√Hz, stability 5×
  better than classical systems (Stray et al., Nature 602, 2022)
- Detection of 70–80 kg human body: gravity signal ~1 nGal at 5 m — at or
  below current instrument noise floor

### Why it cannot replace UWB for real-time human detection

| Constraint | Value | Impact |
|---|---|---|
| Human body gravity signal at 5 m | ~0.5–2 nGal | Below current instrument floor |
| Required averaging time to resolve signal | Minutes–hours | Not real-time |
| Vibration sensitivity | Sensitive to <1 Hz floor vibration | Unusable in active buildings |
| Instrument size / cost | 30–200 kg, $100k–1M | Not deployable |

**Benchmark role**: defines the mass-detection theoretical floor. Quantum
gravimetry proves that any mass (including a human body) leaves a detectable
gravitational signature — the engineering challenge is reducing noise floor
by ~1–2 orders of magnitude and measurement time by ~3 orders of magnitude
before it becomes a viable complement to UWB sensing.

### Rust reference implementation

`poc/rust_kernels/src/gravity_inversion.rs` implements a conjugate-gradient
Tikhonov inversion of the point-mass forward model. It is retained as a
reference kernel and is **not exported** in the PoC pipeline.
